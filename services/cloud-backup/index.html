Recovery, not assumptions

A backup is useful only when the right information can come back.

A green status light does not prove that every important folder, mailbox, or cloud account is protected. We define what the business needs to recover, how far back it may need to go, and how a restore will be requested and verified.

A file is deleted or overwritten

Version history and backup retention provide separate recovery paths when recycle bins are empty or the mistake is discovered weeks later.

A device fails or disappears

We separate the value of the hardware from the value of its data and document how approved files and settings can reach a replacement device.

A cloud account has a wider incident

Independent copies can add protection when deletion, retention changes, account compromise, or provider limitations affect more than one item.

Recovery design

The decisions behind a backup plan you can explain.

We match protection to the cost of lost work. A practical plan distinguishes essential records from replaceable data and sets recovery expectations before an incident creates pressure.

Data map

We identify critical files, mailboxes, cloud locations, workstation data, and systems that may fall outside the obvious shared folders.

Backup frequency

How often information changes and how much recent work the business can repeat guide the protection schedule.

Retention and versions

Daily, weekly, monthly, and long-term versions are balanced against business needs, storage cost, and record obligations.

Restore testing

Representative files and accounts are restored, opened, and checked so the recovery process is demonstrated rather than assumed.

Common questions

Is OneDrive or Google Drive already a backup?+

They provide useful versioning and recycle bins, but separate backup can add longer retention, independent recovery, and protection from account-wide incidents.

How often should recovery be tested?+

The right schedule depends on how often data changes and how costly downtime would be. We recommend a practical cadence and record the results.
